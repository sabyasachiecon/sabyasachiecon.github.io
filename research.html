Household Demand for Sanitation: Importance of Externalities and Borrowing Constraints
Sanghmitra Gautam

Draft forthcoming

This paper develops a method to estimate and simulate household demand for sanitation in the developing world. It analyses the problem of under-adoption of sanitation and addresses a debate among policy makers on the appropriate choice between loans and price subsidy policies to tackle this under-adoption. While sanitation is a costly investment for a poor potentially liquidity constrained household, the choice of adoption has also found to generate health externalities for other households within the village. Both factors may result in under-adoption but are driven by two distinct sources of market failures which act as barriers to adoption. To investigate these two distinct policy effects this project uses a unique dataset on sanitation adoption from rural India. I use the structural model to simulate the effects of different policy interventions and to study the optimal design of such policies in an equilibrium setting, where the recipient household's adoption decision imposes externalities on others. A price subsidy has a high social rate of return where the presence of externalities accounts for a substantial fraction of its impact. While a sanitation loan policy generates smaller social returns is found to be cost efficient under targeted delivery.

Quantifying welfare effects in the presence of externalites: An ex-ante evaluation of a sanitation intervention
Sanghmitra Gautam

This paper analyses the impact of externalities on household demand for a preventive healthcare good and the subsequent welfare effects generated from a subsidy program towards its provision. An interesting feature of such goods is that the take-up generates externalities where the privately chosen adoption level differs from what is socially optimal. Using a unique dataset on sanitation take-up from rural India, I estimate a static demand model keeping into account the interdependence of household decision making within the village. A two-step estimating method is implemented to circumvent the computational burden associated with estimating a model under multiple equilibria. To evaluate the impact of subsidy interventions, I formulate analytical expressions to quantify substitution and income effects in the presence of externalities. Using this tool to further separate out the direct and indirect effects under different policy simulations, I find that substitution effects are significantly larger than income effects, and a substantial amount of this price effect is propagated through the indirect channel. The presence of externalities implies a larger welfare gain, while the Deadweight loss generated from the subsidy intervention is realised as a Net-Gain, as the society shifts towards a socially optimal level of adoption.

Two-step choice probability estimators with measurement error

Sanghmitra Gautam

This paper develops a correction method for existing Two-step methods to estimate static discrete choice models of incomplete information. Under the assumption that only one equilibrium is played in the data, two-step estimators avoid the computational burden associated with repeatedly solving the fixed point for each candidate vector of structural parameters. However, the estimator relies on obtaining consistent choice probability estimates in first step, given that observed data is generated from one of possible equilibria. The problem can be treated as one of contamination of the variable of interest by measurement error. Using insights from small variance approximation to probability distributions, I adapt the error correction method proposed by Chesher (1991) to the estimation of simple static interaction models. The method is applied to estimate Brock & Durlauf's (2001) interaction model which has been the cornerstone in the study of peer effects in recent literature. Monte Carlo simulations are conducted to approximate the magnitude of the impact of error in data and the resulting bias in parameter estimates.
